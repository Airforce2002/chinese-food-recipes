Chinese Cooking Techniques

From the breath of the wok to the precision of knife work, learn the foundational techniques that have defined Chinese cuisine for millennia.

The Eight Essential Techniques

Every dish in Chinese cuisine derives from one or more of these fundamental cooking methods. Master these, and you'll unlock the ability to create authentic flavors at home.

Core Techniques

These eight methods form the foundation of Chinese cooking

01
Bao / 爆

Stir Frying (Wok Hei)

High-heat, rapid cooking in a wok that creates the signature "breath of the wok" flavor. Essential for vegetables, meats, and combination dishes.

  • Heat wok until smoking (300°C / 570°F)
  • Add oil, swirl to coat, heat until shimmering
  • Add aromatics first (ginger, garlic)
  • Cook protein quickly, remove
  • Cook vegetables, return protein, season
02
Zhou / 蒸

Steaming

Preserves nutrients and natural flavors. Used for fish, dim sum, buns, and delicate proteins. The gentle heat keeps ingredients moist and tender.

  • Bring water to full boil before steaming
  • Use bamboo or metal steamers lined with parchment
  • Don't overcrowd — steam circulates
  • Timing starts when steam appears
  • Rest fish 2 minutes after cooking
03
Guo / 锅

Pan Frying

Medium-high heat cooking in a flat-bottomed pan with oil. Creates crispy bottoms while keeping interiors tender. Perfect for dumplings, pancakes, and breaded items.

  • Heat pan first, then add oil
  • Test with water droplet — should dance
  • Don't move food until golden crust forms
  • Use enough oil to coat the pan bottom
  • For dumplings: add water and cover for steamed effect
04
Zhu / 煮

Boiling & Braising

Slow, wet cooking that breaks down collagen and infuses deep flavors. Used for soups, braised dishes, and noodles. Patience is the key ingredient.

  • For braising: sear meat first for flavor
  • Add aromatics and seasonings
  • Use enough liquid to barely cover ingredients
  • Cook low and slow (80-90°C / 175-195°F)
  • Braising improves with resting — reheat to serve
05
Men / 焖

Pressure Cooking

Modern technique that dramatically reduces cooking time while developing deep flavors. Perfect for tough cuts like pork belly and beef that normally need hours.

  • Brown ingredients first for Maillard reaction
  • Add liquid — don't exceed max line
  • Natural release for tough cuts
  • Quick release for vegetables
  • Reduce sauce after cooking if needed
06
Shao / 烧

Red Cooking (Hong Shao)

Slow braising in soy sauce and sugar creates glossy, caramelized dishes. The signature technique for red-braised pork, chicken, and eggs.

  • Use both dark and light soy sauce
  • Add rock sugar or brown sugar
  • Caramelize sauce before adding protein
  • Cook covered on low heat for 1-2 hours
  • Final reduction creates lacquered glaze
07
Zheng / 整

Roasting & Grilling

Dry, high-heat cooking for crispy skin and charred flavors. Used for char siu, Peking duck, and skewered meats. Traditionally done in clay ovens.

  • Dry skin overnight for extra crispiness
  • High heat initially (220°C / 425°F)
  • Glaze during last 20 minutes
  • Rest meat before slicing
  • Char for authenticity in last moments
08
Liang / 凉

Cold Dishes & Salads (Liang Pan)

Served at room temperature or chilled. Often dressed with vinegar, sesame oil, and chili. Refreshing counterpoints to hot dishes in a Chinese meal.

  • Ingredients should be cooked and cooled
  • Marinate for 30 minutes minimum
  • Balance: sweet, sour, salty, spicy
  • Add sesame oil last to preserve aroma
  • Serve at cool room temperature
In Depth

The Art of Wok Hei

Wok hei (镬气) — literally "breath of the wok" — is the soul of Chinese stir-frying. It's that smoky, charred flavor that makes restaurant dishes taste so much more exciting than home cooking. Achieving wok hei requires three elements: extremely high heat, a carbon steel wok, and quick, confident movements.

The wok must reach temperatures above 300°C (570°F). Add oil only when the wok is truly hot. The oil should shimmer and flow like water, not pool. Then work fast — every second matters. The motion should be a continuous toss, never a static fry.

Master Chef Tips

🔥

Heat Management

Chinese cooking uses extreme heat. Practice heating your wok until water evaporates instantly. If your exhaust fan can't handle it, use two burners simultaneously.

⏱️

Mise en Place

Everything ready before heat. Chinese cooking happens fast. Chop, measure, and arrange all ingredients. When the wok is hot, there's no time to prep.

🧂

Season in Stages

Salt early, season at end. Soy sauce added to the wok's hot edge (not directly on food) creates deeper flavor through caramelization.
